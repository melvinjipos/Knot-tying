Basic Knots

Overhand Knot

The simplest of the Single-Strand Stopper Knots. The Overhand Knot was described as “the simplest of the Single-Strand Stopper Knots.” It can also be used to prevent the end of a piece of rope unraveling.

  • - Form a loop and pass the end through it.
  • - Tighten it to form the Overhand Knot. When pulled tight it can function as a simple stopper knot.

Square Knot

Simple way to join two ropes made up of two Half Knots. The Square is usually learned when we tie the laces on our first pair of shoes. Admittedly it is usually a bow that we tie – but the underlying knot is a Square (Reef) Knot.

  • - Take two ropes and cross them (red over blue) to form a half knot.
  • - Cross them a second time (red over blue again) and pull the ends tight to form the Square Knot.

Figure 8 Knot

Non-binding, quick and convenient stopper knot. The Figure 8 Knot provides a quick and convenient stopper knot to prevent a line sliding out of sight, e.g., up inside the mast. Its virtue is that, even after it has been jammed tightly against a block, it doesn’t bind; it can be undone easily. This virtue is also, occasionally, a vice. The Figure 8 Knot can fall undone and then has to be retied.

  • - Pass the tail over itself to form a loop.
  • - Continue under and around the standing end.
  • - Complete the knot by passing the tail down through the loop.

Sheet Bend

Joins two ropes of unequal, or similar, size. The Sheet Bend is recommended for joining two ropes of unequal size. The thicker rope must be used for the simple bight as shown. However, it works equally well if the ropes are of the same size.

  • - Form a bight in the thicker rope (blue) and hold it in one hand.
  • - Pass the thinner rope (red) through the bight and behind the (blue) tail and standing ends in that order.
  • - Finally, tuck the smaller rope under itself to finish the knot.

Slip Knot

Simple loop in rope's end - loosens when tail end is pulled. The Slip Knot is identical in structure to the Noose Knot except that the bight to be inserted is formed from the short end – not the long. The Slip Knot is one of the most frequently tied knots – being used in knitting as the first loop when casting on – where it is called a slip knot but frequently tied as a noose. It can be used as a temporary stopper knot.

  • - Form a loop in the end of the rope.
  • - Prepare a bight in the short end.
  • - Tuck the bight through the loop and tighten.
  • - The knot can be used as temporary stopper knot.

Noose Knot

Creates a loop that tightens when pulled. The simple Noose Knot is identical in structure to the Slip Knot except that the bight to be inserted is formed from the long end – and not the short one. It has been used as a snare to catch small animals.

  • - Form a loop in the end of the rope.
  • - Tuck a bight of the standing end through the loop.
  • - Make the bight larger and pass it around the object.
  • - Pull on the standing end to tighten the noose.

Half Hitch Knot

Used to tie rope around an object and back to itself. It is tied with one end of a rope being passed around an object and secured to its own standing part with a Single Hitch

  • - Form a loop around the object.
  • - Pass the end around the standing end and through the loop.
  • - Tighten into a Half Hitch which is designed to take a load (Arrow) on the standing end.

Half Knot

Simple binding knot: first step of the Square (Reef) Knot. Described the Half Knot as a binding knot, being the first movement of the Reef or Square Knot.

  • - Cross the two ends over each other.
  • - Pass the end around the standing end and through the loop.
  • - Pass one end over and then under the other to make the Half Knot - which takes the load (arrows) in both ropes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is it important to learn basic knots?

Learning basic knots is essential for various practical reasons. Knots are used in daily life, from tying shoelaces to securing items, as well as in specialized activities like camping, sailing, and rescue operations. Knowing the right knot can improve safety, efficiency, and success in many situations.

Are there any safety concerns when using knots?

Yes, safety is a concern when using knots, especially in activities like climbing or rescue. Ensure the knot is tied correctly, tested before use, and suited for the task. Avoid knots that may slip or become too tight. In high-stakes situations, practice regularly and confirm the knot can safely handle the required load.

How can I practice tying knots effectively?

Start by practicing with a piece of rope or cord. Follow step-by-step instructions or watch video tutorials to ensure you are tying the knots correctly. Repetition is key; practice each knot multiple times until you can tie it quickly and without reference. Using real-world scenarios, like tying knots during camping or securing objects, can also reinforce your skills.

Are there specific knots I should avoid as a beginner?

As a beginner, avoid overly complex knots or those that are hard to untie, such as the Double Fisherman’s Knot or the Rolling Hitch. Start with simpler knots like the Square Knot or Bowline, which are easier to learn and apply. Once you master the basics, you can explore more advanced knots for specialized uses.

Can knots become untied over time, and if so, how can I prevent this?

Yes, knots can become untied over time, especially when subject to movement, tension, or environmental factors like water or friction. This is particularly common with certain materials like nylon, which tends to be slippery. To prevent this, you can use knots that are designed to stay secure under tension, such as the Bowline or Figure-Eight Knot. Additionally, adding an extra half-hitch or a stopper knot, like the Double Overhand, can provide extra security and reduce the likelihood of the knot loosening over time.
